Module 0 — Grant Readiness Assessment

Before we open a single AI tool or write a single word of your proposal, we need to answer one critical question: Are you actually ready to receive funding?

Your Readiness Score: 0 out of 19

This assessment is a guide, not a gate. Answer based on your specific organization type. If a question does not apply to your situation, mark it as Yes and continue forward. Your score gives you a strategic recommendation — it does not restrict your access to any module. All seven modules are available to you immediately regardless of your score.

Area 1 — Legal and Organizational Status

1. Do you have official 501(c)(3) nonprofit status or a registered business entity (LLC, S-Corp, C-Corp)?

2. Do you have an active Employer Identification Number (EIN)?

3. Do you have an active, functioning Board of Directors or an official advisory structure?

4. Are your governing documents (bylaws, articles of incorporation, or operating agreements) finalized and accessible?

Bylaws are the rules that govern how your organization operates — who can vote, how decisions are made, how leadership is selected. Templates are available free online from most state nonprofit associations.

Area 2 — Program Documentation

5. Do you have a written, standardized description of the specific program or project you want to fund?

6. Have you clearly defined your target population, including specific demographic data?

7. Are your program activities and implementation timelines clearly documented?

8. Do you have a clear Theory of Change or Logic Model explaining how your activities lead to specific outcomes?

A Theory of Change is a simple diagram or written statement that explains how your program activities lead to specific outcomes. For example: We provide job training which leads to employment which leads to reduced poverty in our community.
Inputs -> Activities -> Outputs -> Outcomes -> Impact

Area 3 — Outcome Tracking

9. Do you consistently track program participation numbers and attendance?

10. Do you collect pre- and post-program data, surveys, or assessments from your participants?

This means measuring your participants before your program starts and again after it ends. For example giving a survey on day one and the same survey on the last day to show what changed.

11. Have you documented specific success stories or detailed case studies of your impact?

12. Do you have at least one full cycle of outcome data ready to report to a funder?

A full cycle means you have run your program from start to finish at least once and have real numbers to show what happened. For example completing one full 12-week cohort and documenting the results.

Area 4 — Financial Records

13. Do you have a current, board-approved organizational budget for this fiscal year?

Board approval means the budget was voted on by the board at the start of the fiscal year. It should be a simple income and expense spreadsheet, and a simple Google Sheets template works fine for small organizations.

14. Do you have a separate, detailed program budget specifically for the work you want funded?

15. Can you produce clean bank statements or formal financial statements (P&L, Balance Sheet) from the past 12 months?

16. If your revenue requires it, have you completed an independent audit or financial review?

An independent audit is a formal review of your finances by a certified accountant who is not part of your organization. Many foundations only require this if your annual revenue exceeds $500,000. If you are a small organization this question may not apply to you — mark it Yes and continue.

Area 5 — Organizational Capacity

17. Do you have dedicated staff members or committed volunteers assigned to manage grant execution?

This does not require a full-time grant writer. Even one part-time volunteer who is responsible for tracking deadlines and submitting reports qualifies. What funders want to see is that someone is accountable for compliance.

18. Do you have a reliable system in place for tracking reporting deadlines and compliance requirements?

19. Does your accounting system have the ability to track and manage restricted grant funds separately from general operating funds?

Restricted funds are grant dollars that must be spent only on the specific program the funder approved. Your accounting system needs to be able to show exactly how those dollars were spent separately from your general operating budget. QuickBooks, Wave, and even a dedicated spreadsheet can handle this.

Before We Begin — Understanding AI Tools

If you have never used an AI tool before, or if you have only experimented with it casually, this section is for you. Do not skip it. Understanding how to communicate with AI is the foundation of this entire system. Students who take 10 minutes to read this section get dramatically better results from every prompt in the course.

What Is AI and How Does It Work For Grant Writing?

Artificial Intelligence tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Perplexity are language tools that respond to written instructions. You type a request, they generate a response. The quality of what they produce depends almost entirely on the quality of the instructions you give them. Think of it like hiring a highly educated assistant who knows nothing about your organization yet. The more clearly and specifically you explain what you need, the better the work they produce. This is why the AI Grant Vault does not just hand you a list of prompts — it teaches you how to structure those prompts so the AI produces professional, strategic, grant-ready content every single time.

What Is a Prompt?

A prompt is simply the instruction you type into an AI tool. It can be one sentence or several paragraphs. The more context and direction you provide in your prompt, the better the AI's response will be. A weak prompt produces weak output. A strategic prompt produces professional output. This course gives you pre-built prompts for every stage of the grant writing process, but understanding why they are structured the way they are will help you adapt them to your specific situation.

See an example of a weak prompt versus a strong prompt

Weak prompt: "Write me a grant for my nonprofit."

Why it fails: The AI has no idea who you are, what you do, who you serve, how much money you need, or what funder you are applying to. It will produce something generic that no funder will take seriously.

Strong prompt: "Act as a Master Grant Writer with a 95 percent win rate. I am applying for a $25,000 community health grant from the Smith Family Foundation. My organization provides free weekly nutrition education to low-income seniors aged 65 and older in Atlanta, Georgia. We have served 312 seniors since 2022 and reduced emergency room visits among participants by 18 percent according to our 2024 impact report. Write a 250-word executive summary that leads with the problem, presents our solution and timeline, and ends with exactly how the $25,000 will be used and what measurable outcome it will produce."

Why it works: The AI now knows your role, your funder, your target population, your geography, your track record, your data, and your exact output requirement. It will produce something a reviewer can actually score.

The Hamburger Method — How to Structure Any AI Prompt

Every great AI prompt has three layers, just like a hamburger. When you build your prompts this way you will get professional results every single time.

THE TOP BUN — The Role

Tell the AI exactly who it is supposed to be before it starts writing. This sets its entire frame of reference.

Example: 'Act as a Master Grant Writer with a 95 percent win rate who specializes in community nonprofit funding.'

THE FILLING — The Task and Context

This is the most important layer. Tell the AI exactly what you need it to do, who your organization is, who you serve, what data you have, and what the specific grant requires. The more detail you provide here the better the output.

Example: 'Write a 250-word Executive Summary for a grant application requesting $15,000 from the Johnson Community Foundation. My organization provides after-school coding education to underserved youth aged 10 to 14 in Detroit, Michigan. We have served 180 students since 2023 and 87 percent of our graduates passed their school's standardized technology assessment.'

THE BOTTOM BUN — The Quality Standard

Tell the AI exactly what good output looks like. Specify the tone, the length, the format, and anything to avoid.

Example: 'The tone should be professional, data-driven, and urgent but never desperate. Avoid words like groundbreaking or revolutionary. Use short active sentences. Format the response as three tight paragraphs.'

When all three layers are present the AI produces output that sounds like it was written by a professional who has worked with your organization for years. When any layer is missing the output suffers. Every prompt in the AI Grant Vault Master Prompt Library is already built using this three-layer structure. Now you know why.

The System: Sourcing Hyper-Local Grants

Step 1 — Define Your Demographics Before You Search

Before you open any AI tool write down the answers to these three questions on a piece of paper or in a notes document. You will use these answers in every prompt you run in this module.

  • Who exactly do you serve? Be as specific as possible. Include age ranges, geographic location, income level, race or ethnicity if relevant to your program, and any other defining characteristics. Example: Low-income single mothers between the ages of 18 and 35 living in Maricopa County, Arizona.
  • What exactly do you do for them? Describe your core program activity in one sentence. Example: We provide 12-week financial literacy and credit building workshops.
  • What is your legal status and location? Example: We are a registered 501c3 nonprofit based in Phoenix, Arizona, operating since 2021.

Having these three answers written down before you start searching will make every prompt you run significantly more targeted and every result significantly more useful.

Step 2 — Execute the Hyper-Local Search Prompt

Open Perplexity.ai or your preferred AI tool. Start a new search or conversation. Copy the Hyper-Local Scavenger Hunt prompt from your Downloads section, fill in the bracketed fields with your specific information from Step 1, and paste it into the AI.

Important note for Claude users: If you are using Claude paste this prompt directly into your Grant Writing Project so the results are saved alongside your other grant materials.

MODULE 1 PROMPT — Copy This Exactly
Act as an expert nonprofit funding researcher. I need to locate 5 active, currently open grants that fund organizations executing [Insert Core Activity] in [Insert City, County, and State]. Specifically seek out community foundations, corporate giving programs such as banks or local utilities, and family foundations. Do not provide expired grants and do not provide massive national grants like the Gates Foundation. Supply the name of the grant, the deadline, the average award amount, and the direct URL to the application guidelines.
How to fill in the brackets

Replace [Insert Core Activity] with your specific program description from Step 1. Be specific. Instead of youth services write after-school coding education for underserved youth aged 10 to 14. Instead of community health write free nutrition education for low-income seniors aged 65 and older.

Replace [Insert City, County, and State] with your exact service area. Include all three levels of geography — city, county, and state — to give the AI the best chance of finding hyper-local results. Example: Phoenix, Maricopa County, Arizona.

What to expect: The AI should return a list of five or more specific grant programs with names, deadlines, award amounts, and links. If the results are too broad or return national grants instead of local ones add this line to your prompt: Focus only on grants available in my specific city and county. Do not include national foundations or federal programs.

Step 3 — Qualify Every Lead Before You Add It to Your Pipeline

Not every grant the AI finds will be worth your time. Before adding any grant to your pipeline tracker run it through this three-question qualification filter:

  • Question 1 — Geographic match: Does their funding footprint explicitly include your city, county, or state? If their guidelines say they only fund in Illinois and you are in Arizona do not apply. Mark it as a C-Target in your tracker and move on.
  • Question 2 — Deadline viability: Is the deadline more than three weeks away? If the deadline is in less than three weeks you do not have enough time to complete a quality application using this system. Flag it for the next cycle and note the deadline in your tracker.
  • Question 3 — Mission alignment: Read the funder's stated priorities carefully. Does your specific program match what they say they fund? Not loosely match — specifically match. If they fund arts education and you run a job training program that is not a match regardless of how you frame it. Do not waste your time. Move to the next result.

Module 2 — The Pre-Write Matrix

Building the context architecture so the AI writes exactly like your organization.

The Objective:

If you ask AI to "write a grant", it will write a generic, robotic, immediately-rejected proposal. AI needs a Pre-Write Matrix. This is where you feed the AI your organizational DNA.

The System:

  1. The Problem Statement: What specific, measurable problem are you solving?
  2. The Solution: How does your program specifically solve that?
  3. Organizational Capacity: Why are you the best team to solve this? What is your track record?
MODULE 2 PROMPT — The Context Injection Prompt
Act as a Master Grant Writer with a 95% win rate. I will feed you the foundational context of my organization. I do not want you to draft any part of the grant proposal yet. I solely want you to review this, analyze our tone, and state 'Context Accepted. I am ready to draft the proposal.' Organization Name: [Name] Our Mission: [Mission Statement] The Problem We Solve: [Specific, data-backed problem] Our Solution: [How exactly you solve it] Our Track Record: [Key stats, e.g., 'We have helped 500 families since 2020'] Acknowledge receipt of this context.

The Homework:

Complete your Pre-Write Matrix and feed it to the AI to prime your session.

Module 3 — Drafting the Proposal

Generating the four core pillars of a competitive grant narrative.

Pillar 1: The Executive Summary

This is the most important page. If this page fails, the reviewer will not read the rest.

MODULE 3 PROMPT — The Executive Summary Generator
Based on the context I provided, compose a 250-word Executive Summary for a grant application requesting $[Amount] from [Name of Foundation]. The summary must strictly adhere to this structure: Paragraph 1: The specific problem and the target demographic. Paragraph 2: Our proposed solution and the timeline. Paragraph 3: Exactly what the $[Amount] will be utilized for and the anticipated measurable outcome. Tone: Professional, data-driven, urgent but not desperate. Avoid terms like 'groundbreaking' or 'revolutionary'.

Pillar 2: The Background & Need

This is where you tell the data-backed story.

MODULE 3 PROMPT — The Data-Backed Story
Write the 'Organizational Background and Statement of Need' section for this proposal. It should be approximately 500 words. Begin by establishing our credibility and track record. Then, transition to the specific, urgent problem we are addressing right now. Leave placeholders like [Insert Statistic Here] where I need to insert local data to strengthen the argument. Employ strong, active voice.

Pillar 3: Project Description & Objectives

What exactly are you going to do?

MODULE 3 PROMPT — S.M.A.R.T. Objectives
I need to outline the objectives for this project. Translate our core solution into 3 to 5 S.M.A.R.T. (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) objectives. Format them as bullet points. Each objective must explicitly state *who* will be impacted, *what* the outcome will be, *how* it will be measured, and *when* it will occur.

Pillar 4: Evaluation

How do you prove it worked?

MODULE 3 PROMPT — Evaluation Framework
Write the 'Evaluation and Outcomes' section of the proposal. Detail exactly how we will track the success of the S.M.A.R.T. objectives you just created. Describe both the quantitative metrics (e.g., attendance logs, pre/post test scores) and the qualitative metrics (e.g., participant interviews, staff observations) we will utilize. Mention that we will deliver a final impact report to the funder.

The Editing Rule:

Never submit raw AI text. Delete fluff words. Use short, strong, active sentences.

Module 5 — Awarded Grant Reverse Engineering

The strategic advantage: studying real winning proposals instead of guessing.

STEP 1 â€” FINDING AWARDED GRANT DATA

To reverse engineer a win you first need to find the data. Here is the step-by-step system for surfacing awarded grant information.

Source 1 â€” Government Grant Databases

For federal and state funding transparency is mandated by law. Use USASpending.gov and Grants.gov award records to search for your specific grant program. You can see exactly which organizations won, the award amounts, and often the project abstracts that summarize their winning narrative.

Source 2 â€” Foundation 990 Tax Forms

Private foundations must file 990-PF tax returns which are goldmines of competitive intelligence. Use the ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer or the IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search. Pull the most recent 990 for a foundation you want to target. Scroll to the Grants and Allocations section. You will see a line-by-line list of exactly who they funded, for what purpose, and for how much.

Source 3 â€” Foundation Annual Reports

Many medium to large foundations publish digital annual reports or maintain a Past Grantees page on their website. These often include detailed program descriptions and success metrics from the winning organizations.

Source 4 â€” Direct Peer Outreach

The most underutilized method is simply asking. Identify a peer organization in a different geographic region so you are not direct competitors. Ask if they would be willing to share a redacted version of their winning narrative. Most development professionals will say yes when approached respectfully.

STEP 2 â€” THE ANALYSIS FRAMEWORK

Once you have a winning abstract, annual report summary, or full proposal you must analyze it. Do not just read it â€” dissect it using this five-point framework.

  1. The Problem Statement â€” What specific language did the winning organization use to describe the urgency of their problem? What data points did they highlight? Was the framing emotional, data-driven, or a combination of both?
  2. The Solution Framing â€” How did they frame their solution? How did they differentiate their approach from generic interventions? What made their solution sound more credible or more specific than alternatives?
  3. Measurable Objectives â€” What did their success metrics look like? Did they promise broad community changes or highly specific percentage-based outcomes? Note the exact format of how they stated their goals.
  4. Budget Reality â€” How large was their total program budget relative to the award amount they received? Did they ask the funder to cover 100% of the project costs or did they show matching funds or cost sharing? Many funders favor organizations that demonstrate other funding sources.
  5. Funder Alignment â€” What were the funder's stated priorities on their website and how specifically did the winning proposal mirror those exact priorities? Look for language that was almost directly lifted from the funder's guidelines â€” this is intentional and strategic, not lazy.

Step 3: AI Pattern Extraction

Once you secure the text from a winning proposal or abstract, you will use AI to analyze why it succeeded. Paste the winning text into Claude or ChatGPT alongside this prompt:

MODULE 5 PROMPT — AI Pattern Extraction
I am providing you with the text of a successful, funded grant proposal. I need you to act as an expert grant reviewer and reverse-engineer this text. Analyze the text and furnish: 1. The exact tone and reading level utilized. 2. The ratio of data/statistics to emotional storytelling. 3. The specific structure of their problem statement. 4. The recurring keywords or thematic priorities they emphasized. Here is the winning text: [PASTE TEXT]

Module 6 — Building a Grant Pipeline

Shifting from playing the lottery to managing a reliable revenue stream.

STEP 1 â€” BUILDING AND QUALIFYING YOUR TARGET LIST

You cannot build a pipeline with only two targets. You need volume and you need to qualify that volume before you spend a single hour writing.

Sourcing

Use the hyper-local sourcing methods from Module 1 to generate an initial raw list of 25 to 30 potential funders in your geographic area. Do not edit the list yet â€” get the volume first.

The 3-Factor Qualification Score

Evaluate each of the 30 funders using three simple metrics. Geographic Fit â€” Do they explicitly fund in your exact city, county, or state? If their guidelines say they only fund in Illinois and you are in Arizona do not apply regardless of how good the mission alignment looks. Mission Alignment â€” Does your specific program perfectly match their stated funding priorities? Not loosely match â€” specifically match. If they fund arts education and you run a job training program that is not a match no matter how you frame it. Award Size â€” Is their average award size appropriate for your budget? You should not ask for $50,000 from a funder whose average award is $2,000. That mismatch signals that you did not do your research and it will cost you the application.

Ranking Your List

Categorize your raw list into three actionable tiers. A-Targets â€” Highest fit, highest probability. You meet all three factors perfectly. These get applications immediately. Never have fewer than five A-Targets active at any time. B-Targets â€” Good fit but missing one factor. For example mission aligns perfectly but they are slightly outside your primary geography. Worth applying but secondary priority. Schedule these after your A-Targets are submitted. C-Targets â€” Low fit right now. Keep them on a monitoring list but do not waste time applying this cycle. Check back when their priorities shift or when your program expands.

Step 2: AI Target Prioritization

When you accumulate 20 grants in your pipeline, you must determine which ones to attack first. You will leverage AI to rank your opportunities based on ROI.

MODULE 6 PROMPT — AI Target Prioritization
I am providing a list of grant opportunities we are evaluating. I need you to rank them from Highest Priority to Lowest Priority. Base your ranking on these ROI factors: 1. Total funding amount available. 2. Proximity of the deadline (closer deadlines require immediate action). 3. Complexity of the application (a 2-page LOI is higher priority than a 50-page federal application). 4. Alignment with our core mission (which I will supply below). Here is our core mission: [INSERT MISSION] Here are the grant opportunities: [PASTE LIST]

Organize your pipeline into 90-day sprints. Month 1: Sourcing and LOIs. Month 2: Drafting. Month 3: Submission and Cultivation.
